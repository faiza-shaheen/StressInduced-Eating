# Stress-Induced Eating During Examination Periods Among University Students
Type: Literature-based research project  
Field: Public Health / Nutrition  
Year: 2025  
Author: Faiza Shaheen  
## Introduction
University students commonly experience increased levels of psychological stress during examination periods. Academic pressure, time constraints, and performance expectations contribute to heightened stress, which can significantly influence eating behaviors. Stress-induced eating often manifests as emotional eating, irregular meal patterns, increased snacking, and a preference for energy-dense, high-sugar, or high-fat foods. Understanding this relationship is important due to its implications for students’ physical health, mental well-being, and long-term dietary habits.
## Aim
The aim of this project is to review existing literature examining the association between examination-related stress and eating behaviors among university students.
## Methodology
This research project is a narrative literature-based review. Peer-reviewed journal articles were identified through online academic sources and databases. Studies focusing on university students, examination stress, eating behaviors, emotional eating, and dietary changes were included. No primary data collection was conducted.
## Findings from Literature
The reviewed studies consistently report a relationship between increased stress levels and altered eating behaviors among university students. Common findings include increased consumption of snacks, fast food, and sugary foods during examination periods. Several studies highlight emotional eating as a coping mechanism for stress, particularly among students experiencing high academic pressure. Additionally, stress has been associated with meal skipping and irregular eating schedules.
## Public Health Significance
Stress-induced eating among university students represents a relevant public health concern. Poor dietary choices during prolonged stress periods may contribute to weight gain, nutrient deficiencies, and increased risk of non-communicable diseases. Addressing stress management and promoting healthy coping strategies may support better dietary behaviors in student populations.
## Conclusion
Stress-induced eating during examination periods is a well-documented phenomenon among university students. Literature suggests that academic stress significantly influences food choices and eating patterns. Public health interventions focusing on stress reduction, nutrition education, and supportive university environments may help mitigate the negative impact of stress on students’ dietary behaviors.
